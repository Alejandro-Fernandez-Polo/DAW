History
Colonial America and the Revolution (1565-1783)

Between 1565 and 1783, the United States underwent a crucial evolution. In 1565, the Spanish founded St. Augustine in Florida, marking the beginning of European presence. British colonization followed at Jamestown in 1607. Throughout the 17th and 18th centuries, the colonies prospered, but tensions with Great Britain increased due to taxes and restrictions. In 1775, the War of Independence began, led by figures such as George Washington. The Declaration of Independence in 1776 marked the formal separation. The war culminated in 1783 with the Treaty of Paris, recognizing the independence of the United States.

The New Nation (1783-1860)

Between 1783 and 1860, the United States went through an era of growth and conflict. The Constitution was written in 1787, establishing the system of government. Westward expansion accelerated, with the purchase of Louisiana and the Oregon Trail. However, tensions grew over slavery, culminating in the Civil War in 1861. During this period, political parties were formed, Monroe policy was established, and the economy based on agriculture and industrialization developed. The issue of slavery escalated, deeply dividing the country and leading to war.

Civil War (1861-1865)

Between 1861 and 1865, the United States faced its most devastating civil war. The dispute over slavery and state rights led to the secession of the southern states, forming the Confederate States. The conflict erupted in 1861 with the Battle of Fort Sumter. President Abraham Lincoln led the Union, seeking to preserve the union and abolish slavery. The Civil War saw bloody clashes such as the Battle of Gettysburg and Lincoln's Emancipation Proclaimed in 1863. The Union prevailed in 1865, marking the end of slavery with the ratification of the 13th Amendment and the reunification of the country.

Reconstruction and Industrialization (1865-1889)

After the Civil War in 1865, the United States embarked on Reconstruction to heal divisions. The 13th Amendment abolished slavery, but challenges remained. The 14th and 15th Amendments were implemented to grant civil rights and the vote to African Americans. Industrialization emerged, fueled by railroad expansion and economic boom. However, there were labor tensions and conflicts with Native American communities. The gold rush in the West attracted fortune seekers, while racial segregation laws were enacted in the South. The era culminated with the Wounded Knee massacre and the consolidation of industrial power.

The Progressive Era (1890-1913)

Between 1890 and 1913, the United States underwent significant changes. Westward expansion was completed, marking the closing of the frontier. The Industrial Revolution transformed the economy, giving rise to large corporations and social change. Progressive activism emerged, advocating reforms in areas such as labor rights and gender equality. Theodore Roosevelt's administration implemented antitrust and conservationist policies. Immigration increased, contributing to cultural diversity. However, racial tensions and labor conflicts also arose, such as the Pullman strike. The era culminated with the presidency of Woodrow Wilson and the entry of the United States into World War I in 1917.

World War I and the Roaring Twenties (1914-1929)

Between 1915 and 1929, the United States lived through a period of changes and contrasts. Involvement in World War I (1917-1918) marked the beginning of the period. Subsequently, the 1920s became known as the "Roaring Twenties," characterized by economic boom, cultural proliferation and alcohol prohibition. However, prosperity was cut short by the Great Depression in 1929, when the stock market collapsed, triggering a worldwide economic crisis. This period of hardship left a lasting impact on American society and set the stage for subsequent government intervention in the economy.

The Great Depression (1929-1940)

The 1930s in the United States were marked by the Great Depression, triggered by the collapse of the stock market in 1929. Millions of people lost jobs and poverty became widespread. President Franklin D. Roosevelt implemented the New Deal, a set of programs and reforms to combat the crisis. World War II began in Europe in 1939, and although the United States initially remained neutral, its involvement in the conflict from 1941 onward revived the economy and changed the course of world history. The decade ended with the entry of the United States into the war.

World War II (1941-1945)

Between 1941 and 1945, the United States played a crucial role in World War II. Following the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941, the United States entered the conflict. The nation mobilized enormous industrial and human resources to support the Allies. The war in the Pacific and Europe saw decisive battles such as Midway and D-Day. The atomic bomb was used on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945, hastening the end of the conflict. These years marked America's transition to a superpower and the beginning of the Cold War.

The Modern Era (1945-1979)

Between 1946 and 1979, the United States experienced a post-war period that made it a superpower. The Marshall Plan helped European reconstruction, consolidating U.S. influence. The Cold War with the Soviet Union emerged, marked by the arms race and the space race. In the 1950s, society underwent cultural and political changes, including the struggle for civil rights. Involvement in the Vietnam War generated internal divisions. In 1969, Apollo 11 landed on the Moon. The 1970s faced economic, energy and political crises, culminating with the Iranian Revolution in 1979.

Gastronomy
Best restaurants
Yolan

Voted the No. 1 Restaurant in the U.S. by Food + Wine readers, Yolan is an authentic experience of Italian cuisine done right, located at The Joseph Nashville. James Beard Award winner and Michelin-starred chef, Tony Mantuano, and wine and hospitality expert, Cathy Mantuano, craft a true love letter to Italy, while paying homage to the Pizzuti family's heritage and Joseph's wife, Yolanda.

Merois

Wolfgang Puck's Merois, atop Pendry West Hollywood, offers a stylish rooftop dining experience with a Californian twist. Enjoy a sophisticated menu featuring global flavors, from a raw bar and sushi to heartier main dishes with influences from Thailand, Singapore, Japan, and beyond. The breathtaking cityscape serves as the backdrop to this culinary journey, complemented by indulgent house-made desserts and a selection of handcrafted cocktails and fine wines.

Camphor

Camphor is all about easy & relaxed eating, and letting classic French flavors & culinary techniques do the talking. With a menu replete with personal nods to both chefs’ global upbringing, expect a mix of refreshing, refined, yet light fare in a relaxed convivial setting that harkens back to the basics with a shareable feast of small plates, alongside a vast selection of curated wines and curated cocktails to pair.
